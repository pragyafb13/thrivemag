How to Negotiate Your Salary (And Actually Win)

SC
Sarah Chen, Lead Editor
·January 25, 2026

Most people never negotiate their salary. A Carnegie Mellon study found that only 7% of women and 57% of men negotiate job offers — meaning the majority of people accept the first number they're given. Over a 40-year career, failing to negotiate your starting salary can cost you hundreds of thousands of dollars in cumulative earnings. Learning to negotiate isn't optional. It's one of the highest-ROI skills you can develop.

Do Your Research Before Any Conversation

Negotiation without data is guesswork. Before any salary discussion, research the market rate for your specific role, level, location, and industry. Use multiple sources: Glassdoor, Levels.fyi (for tech), LinkedIn Salary, Payscale, and Bureau of Labor Statistics. Talk to peers in your industry. Ask your network what ranges are typical. Arrive at a number that represents the upper-market rate for someone with your exact experience — not just the median.

Your research serves two purposes: it anchors your expectations to reality, and it gives you the confidence to make a specific ask rather than hedging with vague language.

The First Rule: Never Give a Number First

When a recruiter or hiring manager asks "what are your salary expectations?" — deflect. Say: "I'd love to understand the full scope of the role first, and I'm sure we can find a number that works for both sides. What's the budgeted range for this position?" Most of the time, they'll give you a number. That's your baseline to negotiate from, not a ceiling.

If they push back and insist on a number, give a range — but make the bottom of your range higher than your actual minimum acceptable salary. If your target is $95,000, give a range of $95,000–$110,000. Anchoring high shifts the negotiation upward.

How to Counter an Offer

When you receive an offer, don't respond immediately. Take at least 24–48 hours. This gives you time to think clearly, do additional research, and prepare your counter. When you do counter, follow this structure:

  • Express genuine enthusiasm for the role and company
  • State your counter number specifically ("Based on my research and experience, I was hoping for $X")
  • Briefly justify it with evidence (market data, your specific experience, results you've achieved)
  • Stay silent after stating your number — don't fill the silence with concessions

A counter of 10–20% above the initial offer is reasonable in most situations. Be prepared for them to meet you somewhere in the middle.

Negotiating Beyond Base Salary

If an employer genuinely cannot move on base salary — common in government, nonprofit, or companies with rigid pay bands — there's still room to negotiate total compensation. Consider asking for: signing bonus, extra PTO days, remote work flexibility, accelerated performance review timeline, professional development budget, equity or stock options, or title adjustment. These benefits have real monetary value and are often easier for employers to flex on than base salary.

Handling Pushback Without Caving

The most common negotiation failure mode is capitulating the moment you encounter any resistance. Pushback is normal and expected. "Our budget is really tight right now" is a negotiating position, not a final answer. You can respond with: "I understand, and I really want to make this work. Is there any flexibility if I can demonstrate X in the first 90 days?" or "Could we build in a 6-month review with a salary adjustment if I hit these targets?" These responses move the conversation forward without accepting defeat.

Negotiating a Raise at Your Current Job

The same principles apply for current-employer negotiations, with one addition: timing. The best time to ask for a raise is immediately after a visible win — a successful project, strong performance review, or new responsibility you've taken on. Come with data: what you've accomplished, what the market pays for your role, and a specific number. Frame it as recognizing the value you're delivering, not as a personal need. "Based on the results of X and current market data showing $Y for this role in this market, I'd like to discuss adjusting my compensation to $Z."
