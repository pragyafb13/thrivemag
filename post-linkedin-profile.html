Build a LinkedIn Profile That Gets You Noticed

JR
James Rivera, Money & Career Editor
·February 12, 2026

Over 95% of recruiters use LinkedIn to find and evaluate candidates. A weak profile isn't neutral — it actively costs you opportunities you'll never know you missed. A strong profile, on the other hand, works for you 24/7: attracting recruiters, building credibility with prospects, and establishing your professional reputation before you enter any room.

The Headline: Your Most Important 220 Characters

Most people use their job title as their headline. This is a missed opportunity. Your headline appears in search results, next to your comments, and in recruiter searches. It should communicate who you are and what value you offer — not just what you're currently called. Instead of "Marketing Manager at Acme Corp," try "Marketing Manager | B2B Growth | Turning Data Into Campaigns That Actually Convert." Include keywords relevant to your target roles — LinkedIn's algorithm uses the headline heavily for search ranking.

Your Profile Photo Matters More Than You Think

Profiles with a professional photo receive 21x more profile views and 36x more messages, according to LinkedIn's own data. You don't need a professional photographer. You need: good natural light (face a window), a clean background, a genuine smile, and a recent photo where you're clearly visible from the shoulders up. Avoid group photos, vacation shots, or anything where someone else has been cropped out. Dress the way you would for an interview in your industry.

The About Section: Tell a Story, Not a Résumé

The About section is the one place on LinkedIn where you get to speak in first person and show some personality. Don't just list your experience — tell someone who you are, what you care about professionally, and what you bring to the table. A strong About section answers three questions: What do you do? What makes you different? What are you looking for or open to? Keep it to 3–4 short paragraphs. End with a clear call to action: "Feel free to reach out at [email]" or "I'm open to conversations about X."

Experience: Show Results, Not Responsibilities

The most common LinkedIn profile mistake in the Experience section is listing job duties instead of accomplishments. No one is impressed that you "managed social media accounts." They are impressed that you "grew Instagram following from 2,000 to 47,000 in 18 months, driving a 23% increase in inbound leads." For every role, ask yourself: what did I achieve, and can I quantify it? Numbers make you memorable and searchable. Use the formula: "Achieved X by doing Y, resulting in Z."

Skills and Endorsements: Gaming the Algorithm

LinkedIn's search algorithm heavily weights skills. Add up to 50 skills, prioritizing those most relevant to your target roles. Pin your three most important skills to the top. Ask specific people for endorsements for your top 3–5 skills — a brief, direct ask ("Hey, would you be willing to endorse me for product management? Happy to return the favor") has a high acceptance rate. Endorsements from people with strong profiles carry more weight than those from casual connections.

Content: The Fastest Way to Build Visibility

Posting on LinkedIn is the single most underused tool for career growth on the platform. You don't need to post daily. Posting one genuinely useful thing per week — an insight from your work, a lesson learned, a question for your industry — compounds over months into real visibility. LinkedIn's algorithm strongly favors native content (text posts, documents, videos) over external links. The sweet spot for post length is 150–300 words: long enough to be substantive, short enough to be read in a feed.
