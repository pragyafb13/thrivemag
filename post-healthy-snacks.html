25 Healthy Snacks That Actually Taste Good

LT
Lin Torres, Food Editor
· February 10, 2026

Snacking has a bad reputation it doesn't entirely deserve. The problem isn't snacking — it's what we tend to reach for when we're hungry, distracted, and surrounded by ultraprocessed options. The right snacks can stabilize blood sugar, provide micronutrients your meals might miss, and prevent the afternoon crash that sends most people to the vending machine at 3pm.

What Makes a Snack Actually Good for You

A genuinely satisfying, health-supporting snack has three properties: protein (or fiber) to slow digestion, real ingredients you can identify, and enough flavor to feel deliberate rather than desperate. Snacks that check all three boxes will keep you full longer and won't spike your blood sugar the way most packaged options do.

High-Protein Snacks (No Prep Required)

  • Greek yogurt with honey and walnuts — 15–17g protein, creamy, satisfying. Go for plain full-fat; the flavored versions are often as sugary as dessert.
  • Hard-boiled eggs — 6g protein per egg. Make a batch on Sunday and keep them in the fridge all week.
  • String cheese + apple slices — The fat and protein in the cheese slows glucose absorption from the apple. Classic combo for a reason.
  • Edamame with sea salt — Frozen edamame takes 3 minutes in the microwave. 8g protein per half cup. Underrated.
  • Canned sardines on whole grain crackers — High protein, omega-3 rich, shelf-stable. If you've been avoiding sardines, give them one more chance with a squeeze of lemon.
  • Cottage cheese with pineapple — 14g protein per half cup. The sweetness of the pineapple completely transforms plain cottage cheese.
  • Turkey roll-ups — Sliced turkey, avocado, mustard, rolled up. No bread needed, very filling.

Plant-Based Snacks Worth Knowing

  • Hummus and raw vegetables — Carrots, cucumber, bell pepper. The fiber in vegetables + protein in hummus = solid satiety.
  • Almond butter on celery — Sound boring? Add a sprinkle of flaky salt and you'll understand why this is a staple.
  • Roasted chickpeas — Make your own (toss canned chickpeas with olive oil and spices, roast at 400°F for 25 min) or buy them. Crunchy, high-fiber, satisfying.
  • Avocado on rice cakes — Yes, rice cakes. With enough avocado and everything bagel seasoning, they work.
  • Dark chocolate (70%+) and almonds — A 1oz serving of dark chocolate with a small handful of almonds hits fat, magnesium, fiber, and enough sweetness to feel indulgent.
  • Mango slices with Tajín — Sweet, spicy, tangy. Barely any prep. Vitamin C and fiber in abundance.

Snacks for Sustained Energy

If you're snacking to power through a long work session, prioritize snacks that combine complex carbs with protein or fat:

  • Oat energy balls (oats, peanut butter, honey, chocolate chips — no-bake)
  • Brown rice crackers with smoked salmon
  • Banana with 2 tbsp peanut butter
  • Trail mix (make your own: almonds, cashews, dried cranberries, dark chocolate chips — skip the sugar-coated nuts)
  • Overnight oats in a small jar
  • Smoothie with spinach, frozen berries, protein powder, and almond milk

What to Do With the Packaged Snack Aisle

Not every snack needs to be made from scratch. There are genuinely good packaged options — you just need to read labels intelligently. The first three ingredients tell you most of what you need to know. If sugar, high-fructose corn syrup, or refined flour appears in the first three, put it back. Look for options with at least 3g of fiber and 5g of protein per serving, and fewer than 10g of added sugar. A few packaged snacks that clear those bars: RXBARs, Chomps meat sticks, Siete tortilla chips (in moderation), and most plain nut butters.
