Boost Your Credit Score in 90 Days

JR
James Rivera, Money & Career Editor
·February 20, 2026

Your credit score affects the interest rate on your mortgage, car loan, and credit cards — the difference between a 680 and a 760 FICO score on a 30-year mortgage can cost or save you tens of thousands of dollars in interest. Unlike wealth, which takes years to build, your credit score can improve meaningfully in 60–90 days with the right actions.

How Your Score Is Actually Calculated

FICO scores are calculated from five factors, in this order of importance:

  • Payment history (35%): Whether you pay on time. The single biggest factor.
  • Credit utilization (30%): How much of your available credit you're using.
  • Length of credit history (15%): How long your accounts have been open.
  • Credit mix (10%): Whether you have different types of credit (cards, loans, etc.)
  • New credit inquiries (10%): How recently you applied for new credit.

The 90-day playbook focuses almost entirely on the top two: payment history and utilization, because they're both immediately actionable and carry the most weight.

Step 1: Pull Your Free Credit Reports

Go to AnnualCreditReport.com — the only federally authorized free credit report site — and pull your reports from all three bureaus (Equifax, Experian, TransUnion). You're looking for errors: accounts that aren't yours, payments incorrectly reported as late, balances that are wrong, or accounts that should have dropped off after 7 years but haven't. Credit report errors are more common than most people think — a 2021 FTC study found errors in 1 in 5 consumer credit reports. Dispute any errors directly with the bureau online; they're legally required to investigate within 30 days.

Step 2: Slash Your Credit Utilization

Credit utilization — the ratio of your credit card balances to your credit limits — has an almost immediate impact on your score. Scoring models are recalculated every time your card issuer reports to the bureaus (usually monthly). Lower your utilization below 30% and your score will rise in the next scoring cycle. Below 10% will push it higher still.

To lower utilization quickly: pay down balances before your statement closes (not just the due date), ask for a credit limit increase on existing cards (this improves the ratio without requiring you to pay more), or pay multiple times per month to keep balances low when the bureau snapshots your accounts.

Step 3: Never Miss a Payment Again

A single 30-day late payment can drop a good credit score by 60–110 points. Set up autopay for the minimum amount on every account — this ensures you're never late due to forgetfulness, while still letting you pay extra manually when you have it. The autopay minimum is a floor, not a ceiling.

If you have a late payment on your record, it hurts for 7 years, but its impact diminishes over time. The best strategy: build a track record of on-time payments from today forward. Two years of perfect payment history substantially mitigates the damage of older late payments.

What NOT to Do

Several common "credit building" moves can actually hurt your score or waste time:

  • Don't close old credit cards. Closing a card reduces your available credit (raising utilization) and can shorten your average account age. Keep old cards open with small recurring charges on them.
  • Don't apply for multiple new cards at once. Each hard inquiry temporarily drops your score 5–10 points. Space out applications by at least 6 months.
  • Don't pay a "credit repair" company. Anything a legitimate credit repair company can do for you, you can do yourself for free. There is no legal way to remove accurate negative information before its 7-year expiration.

Building Credit From Scratch

If you have no credit history, your best options are: a secured credit card (you put down a deposit that becomes your limit), a credit-builder loan from a credit union or fintech like Self, or becoming an authorized user on a trusted family member's old, well-managed account. All three methods establish a credit history, and with consistent on-time payments, you can reach a 700+ score within 12–18 months from zero.
